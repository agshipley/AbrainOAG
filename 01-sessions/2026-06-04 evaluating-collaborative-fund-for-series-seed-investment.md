## The part to actually negotiate: a board seat on $1.1M

This is where I'd slow down. The fund being reputable doesn't mean the *terms* are automatically founder-friendly, and a board seat is the most consequential thing in the ask.

What's standard: a lead seed investor often does take a board seat, and a three-person board with founders retaining two seats and the new investor taking one is the normal seed structure that keeps founders in control. So the ask isn't outrageous on its face.

But $1.1M is on the smaller side for a full voting seat, and the relevant question is *what percentage* that buys. A common rule of thumb from the investor side: if a VC is buying ~15%, a board seat makes sense; if they're buying 5%, not so much, and seed investors writing smaller checks more typically get an **observer seat** rather than a voting one. Several experienced early-stage investors argue founders shouldn't hand a voting seat to a sub-lead seed check at all, preferring observer rights, which give them a seat at the table without the control.

Concrete things for Erin to push on with a startup lawyer:

1. **Observer vs. voting seat** — if $1.1M is a modest stake, an observer seat gives Collab the involvement they want without a vote.
2. **A sunset / automatic step-off at Series A** — a widely-used founder protection: the seed investor gives up the board seat when the Series A lead comes in. Negotiate that seed investors leave the board at Series A or B, and make sure it's in the investment documents.
3. **Board composition** — keep it 2 founders + 1 investor so founders retain control.
4. **Protective provisions / veto rights** — watch for veto rights disproportionate to a ~10-15% stake; those belong to investors funding most of a round, not a seed participant.
5. **Is this the lead or the whole round?** — confirm whether the $1.1M *is* the full Series Seed or just Collab's slice, since that changes whether a seat is warranted and what dilution looks like. (For reference, median priced-seed dilution is around 20%.)

None of this is a reason to walk — a seat for a constructive, finance-savvy partner from a reputable fund can genuinely help. It's a reason to make the seat *conditional and bounded* rather than open-ended.
