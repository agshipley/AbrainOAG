## The constitutional section

I'd build it around five questions in causal order, because the answer to each narrows the next. Right now the memo asserts a commission is warranted without showing it can be constituted; this is the section that closes that gap.

**1. What kind of body — because the form dictates the Article II analysis.** The realistic forms are a congressional select committee (exercising Congress's own investigative power), a statutory commission seated in the legislative branch (the 9/11 Commission model), an executive commission (the CWRIC model that produced Japanese-American redress), or a DOJ special counsel. The Appointments Clause is the constraint that does the most work: under *Buckley v. Valeo*, anyone exercising "significant authority pursuant to the laws of the United States" is an officer who must be appointed through Article II channels. A body that investigates and recommends, with no binding enforcement power, stays clear of the worst of this — the 9/11 Commission carried mixed congressional and presidential appointments and survived precisely because it wielded no executive enforcement authority. The post-2020 removal cases (*Seila Law*, *Collins v. Yellen*, building on *Free Enterprise Fund*) sharpen the point: this Court is hostile to insulated multimember bodies that exercise executive power. The design implication is that a temporary, investigative, recommendatory commission with a fixed sunset is both the constitutionally safest structure and the one that fits the truth-commission tradition's own self-definition as temporary and non-judicial.

**2. What it can demand, and from whom.** The investigative subpoena power is broad (*McGrain*, *Watkins*, *Eastland*) but bounded by a valid legislative purpose, and *Trump v. Mazars* (2020) imposes a heightened, four-factor balancing test for the President's personal, family, and business records. Subpoenas aimed at the principals have to be drafted to satisfy those factors. Here is where your complicity-beyond-principals priority becomes a constitutional advantage: subpoenas to private third parties — banks, crypto exchanges and on-chain custodians, law firms, donors, contractors — carry no executive-privilege defense at all and face only ordinary privilege objections. The "what happened behind closed doors" record is in many cases more reachable through those third-party channels than through the executive itself. That is worth saying explicitly, since it reframes the breadth of the complicity web as a feature of feasibility.

**3. Executive privilege over a former president — the pivotal contingency.** *United States v. Nixon* establishes the privilege is qualified and yields to demonstrated need; *Nixon v. GSA* holds a former president may assert it but with diminished weight, and that the incumbent's position matters greatly; and *Trump v. Thompson* (2022) is the decisive recent precedent — when the sitting president declines to assert privilege over a predecessor's records, the former president's assertion is weak and the material flows. The honest design implication, which suits your provisional voice, is that a post-2029 commission's access to Trump-era records depends heavily on the 2028 outcome and a successor's willingness to waive. The enterprise is politically contingent in a way the memo should state plainly rather than paper over.

**4. Enforcement — the structural soft spot, and the most attackable claim if left implicit.** Inherent contempt has been dormant since the 1930s. Criminal contempt referrals (2 U.S.C. §§ 192, 194) require DOJ to prosecute, which is hollow when the executive is the target or aligned with it. Civil enforcement means litigating subpoenas one at a time, as in *Committee on the Judiciary v. McGahn*, over years, and the Senate's statutory civil mechanism (28 U.S.C. § 1365) expressly carves out executive withholding on privilege grounds. The realistic leverage is a cooperative successor executive, third-party records that do not require the target's consent, reputational pressure, and enforcement mechanisms built into the enabling statute in advance. A memo that quietly assumes subpoenas are self-executing invites exactly the attack the rest of the document works to avoid.

**5. Self-incrimination, immunity, and the prosecution firewall.** This is where the constitutional analysis and the anti-abuse safeguards are the same problem. Witnesses can invoke the Fifth; Congress can compel testimony with use-and-derivative-use immunity (18 U.S.C. §§ 6002–6005); and *Kastigar* puts the burden on any later prosecution to prove a wholly independent source. The cautionary case is *United States v. North* — immunized congressional testimony, aired publicly, tainted the subsequent prosecution and the convictions fell. The design rules follow directly: no general amnesty, no immunity for likely criminal targets without DOJ coordination, a firewall between commission staff and prosecutors, and sequencing so public hearings do not expose grand-jury material or taint juries.

There's a sixth point that bears on your open model question and is easy to miss: the South African amnesty-for-truth bargain is hard to replicate here because clemency is an exclusive Article II power. Congress cannot grant criminal amnesty; only the President can pardon (*Ex parte Garland*, *United States v. Klein*). A "truth for forgiveness" model would therefore require presidential cooperation and could not be legislated. That gives a *constitutional* reason, converging with the normative one, that the prosecution-preserving model sits more naturally in the American system — which informs the open question without foreclosing it.

Specific evidence categories each have a channel worth naming: tax returns run through 26 U.S.C. § 6103(f) and the tax-writing committees (the route upheld in the Ways and Means litigation); grand-jury material requires a Rule 6(e) order; classified material requires cleared staff and negotiated access, since classification authority is core executive power under *Egan*.
