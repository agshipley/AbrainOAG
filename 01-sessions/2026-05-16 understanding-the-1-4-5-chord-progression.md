Here's how it works.

In any major key, you can build a chord on each of the seven scale degrees. You number them 1 through 7. The 1, 4, and 5 chords are the three *major* chords in a major key, and they happen to be the harmonic backbone of countless songs.

In the key of **C major**, the scale is C–D–E–F–G–A–B. Building triads on the 1st, 4th, and 5th degrees gives you:

- **I** = C major (C–E–G)
- **IV** = F major (F–A–C)
- **V** = G major (G–B–D)

So a I-IV-V progression in C is simply **C → F → G**.

Why these three chords specifically? Together they contain all seven notes of the C major scale, so they can harmonize any melody in the key. The **V chord (G)** creates the strongest pull back to the **I chord (C)** — that tension-and-release motion is the engine of the progression. The **IV chord (F)** provides a gentler contrast, a kind of departure that isn't as tense as the V.

You'll hear this everywhere: 12-bar blues is built on I-IV-V, and a huge amount of folk, rock, country, and pop ("Twist and Shout," "La Bamba," "Wild Thing," and so on) leans on it. A common arrangement is I–IV–V–I, which gives you departure, more departure, tension, and resolution.
