## The conceptual setup the literature actually uses

The historical opposition is Housing First (HF) versus what's variously called "Treatment First," "linear continuum," or "staircase" models. The staircase model — dominant from the 1980s through the early 2000s — required people to ascend through stages (shelter → sobriety → transitional housing → permanent housing), with each step contingent on demonstrating "housing readiness." Pratt's position is essentially a 2020s revival of the staircase logic.

But two crucial distinctions get conflated in the political debate:

**Program model vs. system principle.** "Housing First" originally denoted a specific clinical-programmatic intervention developed by Sam Tsemberis and Pathways to Housing in early-1990s New York: scattered-site permanent housing, Assertive Community Treatment (ACT) team support, low-barrier admission, harm-reduction philosophy, separation of housing and clinical services. The thing called "Housing First" since HUD adopted it as federal policy in 2014 is a much broader allocation principle — prioritize permanent housing over transitional or treatment-conditional placements at the system level. Conflating these is how a contested system-allocation rule gets defended with evidence from tightly-controlled clinical trials.

**Permanent Supportive Housing (PSH) vs. Rapid Rehousing vs. Recovery Housing.** PSH is the long-term, services-rich product for chronically homeless people with disabilities; rapid rehousing is short-term assistance for transitional homelessness; recovery housing (the Oxford House model and successors) is sobriety-required group living. Housing First debates mostly concern PSH, but local programs blur all three.

**Voluntary vs. coerced engagement** is a separate axis from housing conditionality. California's CARE Court framework and Proposition 1's $6.4B in behavioral health bonds represent a third path — assisted outpatient treatment leveraged through civil process — that neither Bass nor Pratt is really running on.

## What the evidence actually shows

Three distinct outcome domains have very different evidentiary profiles, and policy debates routinely treat them as one bundle.

**Housing retention/residential stability.** This is where the evidence is strongest and most consistent. There is a robust evidence base demonstrating that Housing First is effective in stably housing a large majority of people experiencing chronic homelessness, including people across the lifespan with mental illness, substance use, chronic medical conditions, frequent ED use, and cognitive or intellectual disabilities. The original Pathways studies showed roughly 80% housing retention at 24 months versus ~30% for treatment-first comparators. A direct comparison of HF vs. TF clients with co-occurring serious mental illness and substance use found the HF group had significantly lower rates of substance use and substance abuse treatment utilization, and were significantly less likely to leave their program — contrasted with the difficulties TF programs have in retaining clients. The dropout problem is the staircase model's Achilles' heel: people fail out at every transition, often back to the street.

**Substance use and behavioral health symptoms.** Here the evidence is mixed and far weaker. A 2019 Baxter et al. systematic review and meta-analysis of RCTs found the impact of HF on most short-term health outcomes was imprecisely estimated, with varying effect directions, and no clear difference in substance use was seen. Note carefully what this does and does not say: HF doesn't reliably *reduce* substance use, it just doesn't make it worse than TF does, and TF doesn't reliably produce sobriety either.

**Broader health outcomes, mortality, "improvement."** This is the weakest layer and the one most overstated in advocacy. The 2018 National Academies (NASEM) report is the most rigorous consensus assessment and it landed harder on the skeptical side than HF advocates typically acknowledge. The committee found many limitations in the existing research base — inconsistent definitions of PSH, limited follow-up periods, and data systems not designed to integrate homelessness and health data — that precluded strong conclusions about the impacts of PSH on health. RCTs considering general measures of health status found no improvement in health status with a move into housing. Recent reporting summarizes the synthesis bluntly: NASEM found "no substantial evidence" that PSH "improved health outcomes, notwithstanding the intuitive logic that it should"; The Lancet found "no measurable effect" on the severity of psychiatric issues or substance abuse; another academic survey concluded PSH produced "no additional health benefit" over other programs. Multiple studies, including evaluations in Canada, Santa Clara County, and Denver, found no statistically significant difference in mortality rates between HF participants and people receiving other services.

## The major case studies, honestly assessed

**At Home/Chez Soi (Canada, 2009-2013).** This pan-Canadian $110 million pragmatic RCT, funded by the Mental Health Commission of Canada, ran in five cities — Vancouver, Winnipeg, Toronto, Montréal, and Moncton — and is the largest RCT of Housing First ever conducted. It enrolled adults with serious mental illness experiencing chronic homelessness. HF improved housing stability, quality of life, vocational opportunities, and community integration over treatment first or usual care — but less so for substance abuse outcomes. The trial is methodologically the gold standard. Its finding is essentially "HF wins on retention and quality of life; clinical outcomes converge."

**Pathways to Housing (NYC, original).** Tsemberis's foundational RCT in the 1990s and follow-ups established the housing-retention finding and seeded HUD's eventual adoption. Strong internal validity but small samples and an unusual population (chronic homeless with psychiatric disability in NYC's specific housing market).

**Finland (2008-present).** The most-cited international case. Since the launch of Housing First in 2008, the number of homeless people in Finland has decreased by 30 percent, and the number of long-term homeless people by more than 35 percent; some sources put long-term homelessness reduction at 75%+. PAAVO I (2008-2011) had the objective of halving long-term homelessness using HF; PAAVO II sought to eliminate it. Until 2008, Finland followed the widely-used staircase approach, which did not prove successful in eliminating long-term homelessness. But the Finnish program is structurally not what LA does. Finland's HF relies more on congregate (purpose-built) housing than scattered-site, partly because only 19% of Finland's housing stock is private rental units. Finland applies HF to everyone, whereas the US tends to reserve PSH for people with mental illnesses and other disabilities. Equally important and rarely mentioned: Finland's homeless population is smaller, less concentrated in open-air drug markets, far less affected by fentanyl, and embedded in a Nordic welfare state with universal healthcare and stronger income supports. Cross-national transfer is messy.

**Salt Lake City / Utah (2005-2025).** The most cautionary case for HF advocates. Utah was held up as the proof of concept — the conservative state that solved chronic homelessness. When the 2005 plan came to an end in 2015, Utah made national headlines, boasting a 91% reduction in chronic homelessness. Utah's success inspired imitation across the country. But the headline number was largely a measurement artifact: the method for determining chronic homelessness changed twice during the decade, corresponding to the two largest single-year drops. Before 2010, the state mistakenly counted people in transitional housing as chronic. Although they fixed the error going forward, they did not adjust their estimates for the previous years, artificially producing the appearance of a significant decline. In 2015, the Salt Lake region reported a staggering decline attributed to PSH expansion and "a new process of confirming whether individuals are chronically homeless." Since the region had only added 34 PSH beds, the lion's share came from the methodology change. Utah officials themselves now acknowledge: "as far as our chronically homeless, we've never gotten there". Utah is now leading a shift away from Housing First under the Trump administration, concluding that "homelessness is not solved by the housing first strategy Utah pioneered from 2005-2015. Services without strict standards might make the problem even worse".

## The serious critique of Housing First

Stephen Eide of the Manhattan Institute is the most rigorous critic and worth taking seriously even if you disagree with his conclusions. His core arguments:

Proponents of HF claim it has been "proven" to end homelessness, but implementation in places like California has failed even to arrest the increase. Proponents have overstated the evidence behind HF's ability to end homelessness, its cost-effectiveness, and its ability to improve the lives of the homeless.

The evidence for HF and behavioral health is far weaker than for residential stability. Some HF proponents, committed to the harm-reduction philosophy of recovery as a choice, are forthright about HF's modest ability to address behavioral health disorders. More commonly, though, advocates display a rhetorical suggestiveness about the link between permanent housing and behavioral health that seems intended to convince the public of evidence that does not exist.

The 2015 HUD Family Options Study showed robust rates of residential stability for families receiving permanent housing intervention. But it also found that housing subsidies, instead of granting recipients the freedom to focus more on employment, actually led to diminished work effort. Housing subsidies increased rates of housing stability — at a greater cost than other interventions — but not self-sufficiency.

The strongest form of Eide's argument is not "HF doesn't work" — it's "HF works for what it actually does (keeping disabled chronically homeless people in housing), and the political class oversold it as a community-level solution to homelessness, behavioral health, and self-sufficiency, none of which it has demonstrated."

The fair counter is that the critique sometimes attacks a strawman. As Urban Institute's Samantha Batko notes, treatment-first models have not been studied nearly as rigorously, and direct comparisons show they house fewer people: "There's nothing that shows that what they're putting forward works". The honest summary is that we have decent evidence for one approach on one outcome (HF on housing retention), modest evidence on others, and essentially no rigorous evidence that any compulsory or contingency-based model produces better outcomes on anything — including the sobriety it ostensibly prioritizes.

## What's actually driving the current policy realignment

A few things have changed since the 2014 HUD adoption of HF:

1. **Fentanyl.** The drug supply post-2014 is not the drug supply during the foundational HF studies. The chronically homeless population in 2025 LA is using substances of vastly higher lethality and addictive potential than the population in 1990s NYC where Pathways was developed. This is a real methodological problem for relying on the legacy literature.

2. **Visible street disorder.** HF was developed for a population largely indoors in marginal housing (SROs, shelters, doubled up). The current political crisis is about visible encampments, which HF was never specifically designed to address and which it does not, by itself, resolve.

3. **Housing supply.** The original HF studies assumed available housing stock. As one critique notes: "What failed was the political will to build and fund housing at the scale required. Replacing an imperfect but evidence-supported approach with largely untested alternatives will not fix that underlying failure". In a market with no slack, "Housing First" just reshuffles the queue.

4. **Federal direction shift.** The Trump administration is moving roughly 60% of federal homelessness aid away from HF priority and toward treatment-first models — though skeptics note treatment-first lacks comparable evidence. Utah, Georgia, and Florida are leading this realignment at the state level.

## The intellectually honest bottom line

The PhD-level summary is that the academic literature supports Housing First on its narrowest claim (residential stability for chronically homeless people with disability), gives qualified support on substance use (HF performs at least as well as TF, neither produces dramatic improvement), and does not support the broader claims that either model resolves community-level homelessness, improves health outcomes substantially, or saves system-wide costs in ways that would have been politically promised in 2010-2014. The Bass-Pratt debate is largely a debate about which oversold paradigm to organize spending around. ## What the peer-reviewed RCT literature actually shows

The foundational study is **Tsemberis, Gulcur & Nakae (2004) in the American Journal of Public Health**. Two hundred twenty-five participants were randomly assigned to receive housing contingent on treatment and sobriety (control) or to receive immediate housing without treatment prerequisites (experimental). Critically, despite the TF group using more substance abuse treatment, there was no significant between-group difference on drug use (p=.42). That last result is the load-bearing finding: requiring sobriety and forcing treatment produced *no* substance use advantage at 24 months, while substantially reducing housing access and retention.

The four-year follow-up (Padgett, Gulcur & Tsemberis 2006, *Research on Social Work Practice*) sharpened the conclusion. After 48 months, results showed no significant group differences in alcohol and drug use. Treatment first participants were significantly more likely to use treatment services. The 2011 follow-up (Padgett et al., *Community Mental Health Journal*) found that HF clients were significantly less likely to use or abuse substances when compared to TF clients, far less likely to use substance abuse treatment services, and far less likely to drop out of services. Having the security of a place to live appears to afford greater opportunities and motivation to control substance use when compared to the available alternatives of congregate residential treatment or a return to the streets.

The **At Home/Chez Soi trial** (multiple papers, *BMJ Open*, *PLoS One*, *Lancet Psychiatry*, 2013-2017) — the largest HF RCT ever conducted, with over 2,000 participants across five Canadian cities — replicated the housing-retention finding decisively. A 2013 RCT analysis in PLoS ONE further found Housing First reduced re-offending among formerly homeless adults with mental disorders, the first RCT to demonstrate HF benefits in the domain of public safety and crime.

The **Aubry et al. (2020) systematic review and meta-analysis in *Lancet Public Health*** is the most authoritative recent synthesis. ## What the peer-reviewed evidence on Treatment First actually shows

This is where your intuition is largely right, but with one important wrinkle. There isn't really a robust body of RCT literature establishing that pure TF "works." The historical staircase model was never rigorously evaluated as a system because it was simply the default; HF emerged precisely because clinicians watched dropout cascades occur at every TF transition point. The peer-reviewed comparative trials we have are essentially all HF-versus-TF studies, and they consistently favor HF on housing outcomes and find no TF advantage on substance use.

**The one wrinkle worth knowing about**: the **Milby/Birmingham Homeless Cocaine Studies** (1990-2006, multiple papers in *AJPH*, *Drug and Alcohol Dependence*, *Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology*). First, the effect didn't persist. The 12-month follow-up found within-group improvements but group differences for drug abstinence found in the earlier Milby paper failed to persist at 12 months. Short-term contingency effects faded.

Second, the population reachable by the program was small. In Birmingham, the abstinence requirement at the time of this trial was 12 continuous weeks, a standard met by 21.7% of participants. Roughly four-fifths of the eligible chronically homeless cocaine-dependent population could not actually access the housing under contingency rules — which is exactly the dropout/exclusion problem TF was already known to have.

Third, this is pre-fentanyl, cocaine-specific, and pre-MAT-normalized. The drug supply and clinical environment have changed fundamentally. The 2017 cost-effectiveness analysis (Mennemeyer et al., *J Mental Health Policy & Economics*) concluded populations treated by Housing First programs may differ from the Birmingham Homeless studies in the severity of addiction or co-occurring psychological problems. Abstinent-contingent safe housing with counseling can substantially improve abstinence for homeless cocaine abusers. Incremental costs rise sharply with more intensive counseling; modest programs of counseling may be more cost effective in a stepped treatment strategy. That's a modest peer-reviewed defense of contingency management, not a vindication of "Treatment First" as an organizing principle for a city's homelessness system.

The **Oxford House recovery housing literature** (Jason et al., multiple studies in *AJPH*, *Journal of Community Psychology*, *Community Mental Health Journal*) is the other peer-reviewed island of TF-adjacent evidence. Individuals randomized to abstinence-contingent housing (Oxford House) exhibited superior outcomes at 24 months compared to individuals assigned to usual care (outpatient treatment or self-help groups). Compared to usual care, individuals in the housing condition exhibited significantly lower substance use (31.3% vs. 64.8%), significantly higher monthly income ($989 vs. $440) and significantly lower incarceration rates (3% vs. 9%). A 2014 Psychiatric Services review found the evidence base for recovery housing limited but positive: two years after entering the Oxford House, individuals had significantly less substance use, more employment, and higher incomes than those who received usual care.

But — and this is the crucial qualifier — Oxford House is not Treatment First in the policy sense. It's a peer-run sober-living model serving people who have already completed treatment and chosen abstinence. In the Oxford House model, residents share in decision making, house management, and informally support one another. The population is self-selected and post-treatment. It's a step-down resource for motivated abstainers, not an intervention for chronically homeless people with active untreated SUD living on the street. Citing Oxford House as evidence for Pratt's plan is a category error — they're solving different problems for different people.

## The assumption, stated precisely

The intuition underlying the restrictionist position is a textbook partial-equilibrium model: labor demand slopes downward, immigrants shift labor supply to the right, so the equilibrium wage falls — and falls most for the native workers who most closely resemble the immigrants (the low-skilled). This is analytically coherent and, within its own assumptions, correct. The reason it has not survived contact with the data in the simple form is that it holds three things fixed that are not, in fact, fixed: labor *demand*, the degree to which immigrants and natives are *substitutes*, and the *capital stock and technology*. Each of those is an escape hatch, and the empirical literature is essentially a 35-year argument about how large each one is.

The deepest version of the error is what economists call the "lump of labor" fallacy — treating the number of jobs as fixed. Immigrants are not only units of labor supply; they are also consumers who buy housing, food, and services (shifting labor *demand* right), and they are inputs whose presence changes what gets produced and how. As the Brookings summary of the enforcement literature puts it, the prevailing older view was that foreign- and native-born workers are substitutes — one immigrant job means one fewer native job — and that is precisely the premise the recent evidence undercuts.

## The theoretical battleground: substitutes or complements?

Everything hinges on the elasticity of substitution between immigrant and native labor *within* a skill group. If they are perfect substitutes, the simple model is roughly right. If they are imperfect substitutes — because of language, specialization, or different comparative advantages — then immigrants can *raise* demand for complementary native labor.

The complementarity position is associated with Ottaviano and Peri. Their work found that immigrant and native workers are not perfect substitutes within narrowly defined skill groups, which has the important policy implication that immigration may raise the wages of many native-born workers. In their model, allowing for imperfect substitution and full capital adjustment, natives gain on average through immigrants providing complementary skills, with even the least-educated native workers gaining modestly in some specifications — though these are long-run, model-dependent magnitudes.

Borjas, Grogger, and Hanson mounted the central rebuttal, and it is a serious one. They showed that Ottaviano and Peri's finding of imperfect substitution is fragile and depends on how the sample of workers is constructed — the complementarity result evaporates simply by removing high-school students from the data, because under the Ottaviano-Peri classification currently-enrolled juniors and seniors are counted among high-school dropouts, inflating the count of young low-skilled workers. Their conclusion: once standard methods are used, one cannot reject the hypothesis that comparably skilled immigrant and native workers are perfect substitutes. This is not a fringe view — Borjas is the most-cited labor economist on immigration, and the disagreement is genuine and methodological, not ideological.

Borjas's affirmative contribution is the "national skill-cell" approach (Borjas 2003, QJE). Rather than comparing cities — which is contaminated because natives move away from high-immigration cities, dispersing the effect — he compares education-by-experience cells at the national level, and finds that immigration-induced supply increases reduce the wages of competing native workers, on the order of a 3–4% wage decline for a 10% supply increase in a skill cell.

## The empirical battleground: the Mariel Boatlift

This single 1980 episode — the sudden arrival of ~125,000 Cubans in Miami, ~half of them high-school dropouts — has become the field's defining natural experiment, and the three-way fight over it is the cleanest window into why economists disagree.

Card (1990) started it: aggregating all workers with a high-school education or less, he found the boatlift did not affect native wages or unemployment in Miami. For decades this was the canonical "immigration has no wage effect" result.

Borjas (2017) overturned it: separating out male non-Hispanic workers with less than a high-school education, he found a large and robust fall in average wages for that specific group in Miami relative to comparison cities after 1980 — a drop of 10 to 30 percent depending on the placebo group. His methodological argument is that you must match the skills of immigrants to the natives they actually compete with, and the low-skill group is where the effect lives.

Peri and Yasenov (2019) defended Card using a better control group: applying the synthetic control method to non-Cuban high-school dropouts, they found no significant difference in Miami wages relative to a synthetic control after 1980, and showed that Borjas's large negative result depends on focusing on small subsamples and a short pre-period, where measurement error is severe. In Borjas's specification the Miami sample shrinks to roughly 15–20 observations per year, with measurement error potentially on the order of 20–30%.

Clemens and Hunt (2019) then identified what may be the actual source of the discrepancy — a data artifact: in 1980, coinciding almost exactly with the boatlift, the CPS changed its survey methods to better cover low-skill Black men who had previously been undercounted, so the fraction of non-Hispanic Blacks suddenly doubled in the low-skill Miami subgroup Borjas analyzed. Because Black workers had lower average wages, this compositional shift mechanically depresses measured low-skill wages in Miami right at the treatment date, independent of any immigration effect. This is the kind of finding a litigator appreciates: the dispute resolves not on theory but on whether the instrument measured what it purported to.

The honest read on Mariel is that it cannot bear the weight placed on it by either side — the samples are too small and the data too fragile to deliver a clean verdict, which is itself informative about how modest the true effect likely is.

The National Academies' *The Economic and Fiscal Consequences of Immigration* (2017) is the closest thing to an authoritative synthesis. Its core labor-market finding: the long-term impact of immigration on the wages and employment of native-born workers overall is very small, and any negative impacts are most likely to be found for prior immigrants or native-born high-school dropouts. Both statements are true and come from the same evidence; they differ in emphasis. The "small average effect" and the "real cost to the bottom decile" are not in conflict.

## The most on-point evidence: studies of *undocumented* labor specifically

Because your question is about illegal immigration, the most relevant work isn't the general immigration literature but the natural experiments that *removed* undocumented workers — which test the restrictionist prediction directly: take the workers out, and wages for natives should rise.

The strongest recent study is East, Luck, Mansour, and Velásquez (2023, *Journal of Labor Economics*), exploiting the rollout of Secure Communities, which led to the removal of over 450,000 individuals between 2008 and 2014. They find that the policy reduced the employment share of likely undocumented immigrants by 5.7% — and, importantly, also reduced the employment and wages of U.S.-born workers, with the negative effect on citizens concentrated in middle- to high-skill occupations that complement immigrant labor. This is the opposite of what the simple substitution model predicts: deporting undocumented workers *lowered* native employment, because the two groups were complements, not competitors, and because the lost workers were also lost consumers and lost demand. Related work finds Secure Communities shrank the construction sector (delaying homebuilding) and the childcare sector (reducing the number of college-educated mothers able to work).

The historical analog is even cleaner — Clemens, Lewis, and Postel (2018, *American Economic Review*), studying the 1964 termination of the bracero program, which excluded almost half a million Mexican seasonal farm workers with the explicit stated goal of raising wages and employment for domestic farm workers. The result: they fail to reject zero labor-market impact — bracero exclusion did not measurably raise the wages or employment of domestic farm workers, inconsistent with the simple supply model. The mechanism was endogenous technical change: employers adopted less labor-intensive technologies (the mechanical tomato harvester is the iconic example) and shifted crop mix rather than bidding up native wages. In other words, when you remove the workers, the demand curve for labor moves too — firms mechanize or stop growing labor-intensive crops. (For balance: a published comment argued that substitution between legal and illegal Mexican labor, plus a larger domestic farm-labor pool, may have muted any effect, and questioned whether endogenous technical change fully explains the null — so the bracero null is contested, though the direction is widely accepted.)

These are reinforced theoretically by search-and-matching models — Chassamboulli and Peri and related work — showing that because firms must post and fill vacancies, removing undocumented workers reduces job creation overall, dampening or reversing the wage gains the simple model promises natives.
