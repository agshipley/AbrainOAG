It asks the bankruptcy court to rule that a specific debt owed to a specific creditor cannot be wiped out by the bankruptcy discharge.

Here's why it matters. The whole point of Chapter 7 for Alt is that she walks away with her debts erased. It's essentially a judgment that can never be bankrupted away.

The statute lists specific grounds for non-dischargeability. The ones most relevant to Karalyn's situation are §523(a)(2), which covers debts obtained through false pretenses, false representation, or actual fraud, and §523(a)(6), which covers willful and malicious injury to another person or their property. The argument would be built around Alt's conduct after the settlement: she took on binding financial obligations to protect Karalyn's guaranty exposure, breached those obligations systematically (no extra payments to the SBA loans, retaining operating funds in Recoop, potentially exceeding the salary cap), and then filed bankruptcy to eliminate the consequences — all while Karalyn was sitting exposed on $414K in loan guarantees secured by her home.
