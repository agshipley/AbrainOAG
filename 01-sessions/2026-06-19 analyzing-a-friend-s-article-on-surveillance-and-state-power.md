## The ultimate argument

Strip away the doctrinal scaffolding and Díaz is making one central move: **he relocates the harm of private surveillance away from the individual privacy frame and into the law of public nuisance.**

The dominant way we think about Ring, Flock, Nextdoor, Citizen, etc. is either (a) *government* surveillance law — the Fourth Amendment, *Carpenter*, the third-party doctrine — or (b) *consumer* privacy — FTC actions, state privacy statutes, notice-and-consent. Díaz's claim is that both frames structurally miss the actual injured party. The Fourth Amendment only binds state actors, so it's "wholly inapplicable" to a private camera network. And consumer privacy law protects *the person who bought the device* — not the pedestrian, the delivery driver, the unhoused resident, or the Black jogger who is *captured by* it and has no contractual relationship with anyone. There's a doughnut hole where the people most harmed have no standing in either regime.

His fix is to treat private surveillance as an **unreasonable interference with the public's right to "pass and repass" on public roads** — the paradigmatic, oldest core of public nuisance, going back to physical obstructions of the King's highway. The conceptual innovation is that he argues a *blockage need not be physical*. A herd of doorbell cameras, license plate readers, mosquito alarms, and a hyperlocal app pushing "suspicious person" alerts can obstruct egalitarian access to public space just as effectively as a fence or a hole in the road — by chilling movement and by making the street unsafe or "discomfiting" for some people to use. He grounds the normative claim in a **relational-justice theory** (borrowed from Dagan & Dorfman, and Ripstein's Kant via Neyers): the public sphere should let people move as "substantively free and equal persons," and private surveillance lets a small subset of property owners establish a kind of *super-interest* — a private veto over who belongs and what counts as an acceptable use of the commons.

The harm runs on two tracks. First, **chilling/exclusionary norms** even from perfectly legal conduct — withdrawal from jogging, kids not playing outside, "system avoidance" by people with prior criminal-justice contact (Brayne, Goffman). Second, **individualized injuries with community spillover** — the Florida Ring shooting, the Citizen "Vigilante" manhunt for Devin Hilton — where defamation and vigilante violence make the road unsafe for everyone.

Then Part IV is the practical payload: a **four-factor reasonableness test** (size/scope of the interference; character of the locality; relevant laws/ordinances; applicable standards of care) plus a **responsibility map** for who can be held liable — manufacturers, HOAs, BIDs, neighborhood-watch leads — including a "nuisance due to many" theory (again Dagan & Dorfman) and the *Reckless Associations* idea (Bambauer/Masconale/Sepe) of pinning liability on the most active/culpable nodes in a network rather than every camera owner.

The framework is deliberately **dual-use**: it can power an actual public-nuisance claim *or* be repurposed as a legislative cost-benefit template. He's careful — almost to a fault — to concede that courtroom victory is "unlikely" given tort's hostility to emotional harm and judicial reluctance to declare new public rights, and he leans on Crenshaw and NeJaime to argue the framework has value *even in loss*, as movement-building and harm-documentation.

## Situating it in the literature

The piece sits at the intersection of two bodies of scholarship that don't usually talk to each other, and the contribution is partly in welding them.

**The public-nuisance revival.** Nuisance is, as John Goldberg puts it, "the tort topic of the moment." Public nuisance law is at the center of efforts to hold product manufacturers, energy companies, and internet platforms liable for billions in losses, and scholars have offered competing accounts of its legitimacy and scope. Díaz positions himself among three camps he names explicitly:

- The **expansionists** (David Dana, Leslie Kendrick) who see nuisance as a gap-filler when politics fails.
- The **skeptics** (Thomas Merrill, Donald Gifford, Richard Epstein) who want a "nondynamic" reading so courts don't "devour in one gulp the entire law of tort" and usurp legislatures.
- The **middle/limiting-principle** camp (Goldberg's "On Being a Nuisance," 99 N.Y.U. L. Rev. 864 (2024); Dagan & Dorfman's "Public Nuisance for Private Persons," 74 U. Toronto L.J. 198 (2024); Neyers).

He plants his flag with the third group, and specifically builds on Dagan & Dorfman's relational-justice account. Worth knowing for dinner: Linda Mullenix's *Public Nuisance: The New Mass Tort Frontier* (Cambridge 2024) is the book-length framing of this whole "new public nuisance" wave (opioids, guns, climate, lead paint) — Díaz is essentially asking whether surveillance is the next entry on that list. The skeptics' best shot at him is the one Merrill and the Restatement (Third) make: courts have *rejected* most of these product-based public-nuisance theories, and they'd say data/surveillance is an even worse "fit" than opioids.

**The surveillance/privacy-theory side.** Here he's drawing on the move *away* from individualist privacy toward **collective and relational** framings: Salomé Viljoen's relational theory of data governance, Helen Nissenbaum's contextual integrity, Ari Ezra Waldman's critique of "privacy's rights trap," Jonathon Penney on chilling effects as *social conformity* (not just self-censorship), Karen Levy on how chilling falls unequally on "marked" subjects, and the race-and-space tradition (Carbado's "Strict Scrutiny & the Black Body," Devon Carbado generally, Henderson & Jefferson-Jones's "#LivingWhileBlack," Rachel Godsil's "Race Nuisance," Dorothy Roberts). The originality is using *nuisance doctrine* as the vehicle to carry the relational-privacy critique into a concrete cause of action — most of that literature stops at theory or proposes statutory reform; he gives it a tort home.

It's adjacent to, but distinct from, work like Hartzog & Richards' "Privacy Nicks: How the Law Normalizes Surveillance" (Wash. U. L. Rev. 2024), which similarly pushes for more relational and collective approaches and substantive prohibitions on surveillance tools rather than relying on subjective expectations — but they're working the normalization/expectations angle, not nuisance.

## Applying it to right now — ICE, Flock, Palantir, Trump

This is where the article reads less like theory and more like prophecy, and it's the part most worth talking through tonight, because the facts moved hard in his direction *after* he was drafting.

His central structural claim is that **the public/private surveillance divide "collapses"** — that private networks built "just in case" become an arm of state power through voluntary data-sharing. That's no longer hypothetical:

- **The Flock "side door."** Flock Safety runs ALPRs in 5,000+ jurisdictions. In May 2025, 404 Media revealed that even though Flock's policy bars immigration use, local police ran roughly 4,000+ lookups at the behest of federal agencies for immigration purposes, giving ICE side-door access to a tool it had no formal contract for — officers literally typed "ICE," "immigration," "ERO," "deportee" into the search-reason field. San Francisco police alone let out-of-state agencies run more than a million searches, including at least 19 for ICE.
- **"Front door / back door / side door."** University of Washington's Center for Human Rights documented all three modes in Washington State in 2025 — direct 1:1 sharing with Border Patrol ("front door"), searches by agencies that never authorized them ("back door"), and local users querying on ICE's behalf ("side door"). Senator Wyden separately revealed Flock had run pilots quietly handing data to CBP, HSI, the Secret Service, and NCIS.
- **The KCHA case.** King County Housing Authority's Flock data was used in immigration enforcement searches and in a search for someone who had sought an abortion — the exact "downstream-harms-you-can't-control" scenario Díaz theorizes.
- **The local-revolt response.** At least 30 localities — Cambridge, Eugene, Flagstaff, Santa Cruz — have deactivated Flock cameras or canceled contracts since the start of 2025, citing fears their data feeds a federal deportation dragnet; Bend, Oregon shut its cameras off in January 2026 after federal officials made hundreds of queries through a back door left open by user error. That municipal-deliberation fight is *precisely* the "public deliberation outside the realm of which these networks are built" problem he names — and the localities pushing back are doing, politically, what his nuisance framework would do legally.

And it scales up beyond Flock. The **Palantir "ImmigrationOS"** buildout is the public/private collapse at the federal level: a $30 million April 2025 contract (within a sole-source deal that's ballooned past $145 million) to leverage vast amounts of personal data from private and public entities, plus a March 2025 executive order to "eliminate information silos" and merge data across agencies using Palantir's Foundry, fusing facial recognition, license plate readers, social media, and cellphone metadata to assign threat scores. ICE is also soliciting ~30 vendors for continuous **social media monitoring** for raids.

The connection to Díaz's framework: his whole point is that *individual* privacy law can't touch this because the harmed public has no contractual hook and the Fourth Amendment doesn't reach the private collectors — but a **public-right framing** can name the injury as a harm to the commons. The 2026 reality also stress-tests his framework in a useful way you could raise: his model was built around *neighborhood-scale* HOAs and watch groups, with a deliberate "capacious account of community." When the aggregator is the *federal government* fusing every private feed, does "interference with the right to pass and repass" still capture it, or do you need something bigger? His "nuisance due to many" and *Reckless Associations* threshold (target the most active/culpable nodes) is arguably the most ICE-relevant part — it's a theory for assigning responsibility across a distributed surveillance network without holding every camera owner liable.

One more wrinkle worth flagging to him, lawyer to lawyer: his framework's **"character of the locality"** factor cuts both ways in the current moment. He notes marginalized communities sometimes *deploy* surveillance defensively against police or vigilantes. Under a deportation regime, the same camera that protects a community can be the one ICE queries — which sharpens rather than resolves his reasonableness analysis.

## Deep dive on Ángel's broader project

This article is not a one-off; it's the load-bearing academic statement of a decade-long, remarkably coherent through-line. His stated research agenda is **"the role of law and technology in maintaining racial stratification,"** and everything fits that.

**The Brennan Center years (2018–2021)**, as Counsel in the Liberty & National Security Program, are the empirical and policy substrate for this article. He was lead author or author on:
- *Automatic License Plate Readers: Legal Status and Policy Recommendations* (2020) — literally the ALPR groundwork that resurfaces as the Flock analysis here.
- *Law Enforcement Access to Smart Devices* and *When Police Surveillance Meets the "Internet of Things"* (2020) — the doorbell-camera/IoT terrain.
- *New York City Police Department Surveillance Technology* (2021) and advocacy around the POST Act.
- Earlier commentary warning that face recognition oversight was needed to avoid a "digital stop and frisk," and on holding *private* police partners accountable — you can see this exact article gestating in those op-eds.

He also litigated/advised in this space (amicus and advocacy work with groups like S.T.O.P. on NYPD facial recognition, the Domain Awareness System, etc.). So the nuisance article is a former practitioner-advocate finally building the *theoretical* container for problems he spent years documenting on the ground. That's why Part I reads like a reported feature — he knows the products cold.

**His other major law-review piece, "Online Racialization and the Myth of Colorblind Content Policy," 103 B.U. L. Rev. 1929 (2023)**, is the key to understanding his method. There he argues that colorblind content-moderation policies obscure and legitimize systems of white supremacy — that universal, facially-neutral rules will fail unless they take into account the history and logic of racism, and that colorblind moderation can magnify the advantages of whiteness by making them appear natural while banning minorities who speak out against oppression. It was notable enough to draw an invited response from **Richard Delgado and Jean Stefancic** (the critical-race-theory founders), who called it one of the first applications of CRT to content moderation.

Put the two articles side by side and the intellectual signature is obvious: **facially neutral, "just safety/just policy" systems that quietly encode and entrench racial hierarchy, which individual-rights frameworks are structurally unable to see.** Content moderation in the B.U. piece; private surveillance in the UCLA piece. Both diagnose the same failure mode (colorblindness/individualism as legitimation) and both reach for a *structural/collective* remedy (race-conscious moderation; public-rights nuisance). The surveillance article is the more mature, more doctrinally ambitious of the two — it doesn't just critique, it builds a usable framework.

Smaller related strands: work on social-media content-moderation double standards, the "rise of virtual governments," and "viral inequity" (race/tech/pandemic). All the same family.

## Where this is heading — future pathways

A few live directions, some he gestures at and some the field is pushing:

**1. Nuisance as the litigation vehicle for surveillance and AI harms.** The most concrete near-term path is testing his theory in court against ALPR networks and data brokers, riding the Mullenix "new mass tort frontier" wave. The open question — and it's the field's central debate — is whether courts extend public nuisance to *intangible/dignitary/chilling* harms, or whether the special-injury requirement and the skeptics' "this isn't a tort" objection (Merrill, Restatement Third) hold the line. Expect the next wave of scholarship to fight over exactly that boundary.

**2. Relational/collective data governance as the doctrinal future.** The center of gravity in privacy theory is shifting from individual rights to relational duties — Viljoen's data-governance theory, "duty to system subjects," collective-harm/externality models. Díaz's nuisance framing is one way to operationalize that; fiduciary-duty and structural-prohibition approaches are the competitors. The interesting synthesis nobody's fully written yet is **relational data governance + public nuisance** as a unified theory of networked surveillance harm — which is roughly the gap his article opens.

**3. The federal-fusion problem.** The Palantir/ImmigrationOS reality is generating its own scholarship and litigation (FOIA suits, the EPIC line, ACLU work, state "data minimization" and ALPR-sharing statutes). The frontier question is whether *private-law* tools like nuisance are even the right register once the aggregator is the federal government — or whether this forces a return to constitutional/administrative law, or new federal statutory limits on public-private data sharing. Watch the state legislative response too: Virginia, Illinois, Colorado, California all moved in 2025 to restrict ALPR sharing, and that "relevant laws and ordinances" layer is exactly factor three of his test.

**4. Biometric statutes as the live edge.** He flags that Illinois BIPA, plus Texas and Washington biometric laws, are the rare regimes with teeth (private right of action, collective suits). The growth area is whether biometric-privacy statutes get extended to public-space facial recognition by private actors — which would do statutorily what his nuisance theory does at common law.
