Yes, atonement is a central concept in Judaism, expressed through the Hebrew term *kapparah* (כפרה), which carries connotations of "covering," "wiping away," or "cleansing" of sin. There's a related cluster of ideas worth understanding together.

The most important concept is *teshuvah* (תשובה), usually translated as "repentance" but more literally meaning "return"—a turning back toward God and toward one's better self. In rabbinic thought, *teshuvah* is the primary mechanism by which a person repairs their relationship with God after wrongdoing. Maimonides, in his *Mishneh Torah* (the *Hilkhot Teshuvah*, or "Laws of Repentance"), lays out the classic structure: recognizing the sin, feeling genuine remorse, confessing it verbally (*viddui*), and resolving not to repeat it. For the repentance to be complete, the test is being in the same situation again and choosing differently.

A crucial distinction in Jewish thought is between sins against God and sins against other people. For transgressions between a person and God, sincere repentance and prayer can effect atonement. But for wrongs committed against another human being, *teshuvah* alone is insufficient—you must seek out the person you wronged, make restitution, and ask their forgiveness directly. God does not forgive interpersonal harms on the wrongdoer's behalf; the injured party must be approached first.

The liturgical and temporal heart of atonement is Yom Kippur, the Day of Atonement, the holiest day of the Jewish year. It falls at the culmination of the Ten Days of Repentance that begin with Rosh Hashanah. The day is observed through fasting, intensive prayer, and communal confession of sins (recited in the plural, as a community). In the era of the Temple in Jerusalem, atonement was achieved through a sacrificial system, including the famous ritual of the "scapegoat" (described in Leviticus 16) symbolically carrying the people's sins into the wilderness. After the Temple's destruction in 70 CE, the rabbis reframed atonement around prayer, repentance, and acts of charity (*tzedakah*)—often summarized in the High Holiday liturgy as the three things that avert the severity of the decree: repentance, prayer, and charity.

It's worth noting that the concept differs meaningfully from atonement in some other traditions. In Judaism, atonement is generally not understood as requiring an intermediary or a vicarious sacrifice; the individual bears responsibility for their own return, though communal and divine mercy are essential parts of the framework.
